# Process Calculus Primer

> **Stage**: Struct | **Prerequisites**: [AGENTS.md](../../../../AGENTS.md) | **Formalization Level**: L3-L4

## 1. Definitions

### Def-S-02-01. CCS (Calculus of Communicating Systems)

Milner's CCS (1980) is a label-based synchronous process algebra that laid the syntactic and semantic foundation for the subsequent π-calculus [^1].

**Syntax**:

$$
\begin{aligned}
P, Q ::= &\ 0 \quad \text{(nil / termination)} \\
       |\ &\ \alpha.P \quad \text{(prefix, $\alpha \in \mathcal{A} = \mathcal{N} \cup \bar{\mathcal{N}} \cup \{\tau\}$)} \\
       |\ &\ P + Q \quad \text{(nondeterministic choice)} \\
       |\ &\ P \mid Q \quad \text{(parallel composition)} \\
       |\ &\ P \setminus L \quad \text{(restriction, $L \subseteq \mathcal{N}$)} \\
       |\ &\ P[f] \quad \text{(relabelling, $f: \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{A}$)} \\
       |\ &\ \mu X.P \quad \text{(recursion)}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Core SOS Rules**: CCS introduces structured operational semantics (SOS) with rules [Act], [Sum-L/R], [Par-L/R], [Com], [Res], [Rel], and [Rec]. The handshake communication on complementary names $a$ and $\bar{a}$ produces an internal action $\tau$.

**Intuition**: CCS abstracts concurrent processes as entities communicating via complementary names. The nil process $0$ provides a termination baseline; prefix guarantees prefix closure; restriction $\setminus L$ localizes external actions—this is the formal basis for modularity and information hiding.

---

### Def-S-02-02. CSP (Communicating Sequential Processes)

Hoare's CSP (1985) is a synchronous, statically-named process algebra emphasizing refinement-based verification [^3].

**Syntax**:

$$
\begin{aligned}
P, Q ::= &\ \text{STOP} \quad \text{(deadlock)} \\
       |\ &\ \text{SKIP} \quad \text{(successful termination)} \\
       |\ &\ a \to P \quad \text{(prefix, $a \in \Sigma$)} \\
       |\ &\ P \mathbin{\square} Q \quad \text{(external choice)} \\
       |\ &\ P \mathbin{\sqcap} Q \quad \text{(internal choice / nondeterminism)} \\
       |\ &\ P \mathbin{|||} Q \quad \text{(interleaving)} \\
       |\ &\ P \mathbin{\parallel_A} Q \quad \text{(synchronized parallel on $A$)} \\
       |\ &\ P \setminus A \quad \text{(hiding)} \\
       |\ &\ P; Q \quad \text{(sequential)} \\
       |\ &\ \mu X.F(X) \quad \text{(recursion)}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Semantic Domains**:

- $\text{traces}(P)$: trace semantics
- $\text{failures}(P)$: failure semantics—pairs $(s, X)$ where $s$ is a trace and $X$ is a set of events refused after $s$
- $\text{divergences}(P)$: divergence semantics

**Intuition**: CSP distinguishes external choice $\square$ (environment decides) from internal choice $\sqcap$ (nondeterministic). The explicit synchronization set enables fine-grained control over communication scope.

---

### Def-S-02-03. π-Calculus

Milner et al.'s π-calculus (1992) supports **name passing** (mobility) and is dubbed the "λ-calculus of concurrency" [^2][^6].

**Syntax**:

$$
\begin{aligned}
P, Q ::= &\ 0 \quad \text{(nil)} \\
       |\ &\ a(x).P \quad \text{(input prefix—binds $x$)} \\
       |\ &\ \bar{a}\langle b \rangle.P \quad \text{(output prefix—sends name $b$)} \\
       |\ &\ \tau.P \quad \text{(internal action)} \\
       |\ &\ P + Q \quad \text{(choice)} \\
       |\ &\ P \mid Q \quad \text{(parallel)} \\
       |\ &\ (\nu a)P \quad \text{(restriction / fresh name creation)} \\
       |\ &\ !P \quad \text{(replication)} \\
       |\ &\ [a = b]P \quad \text{(match guard)}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Structural Congruence** $\equiv$ includes commutativity/associativity of $\mid$, scope extrusion for restriction, and $(\nu a)0 \equiv 0$.

**Intuition**: The core innovation is that channels themselves can be transmitted as messages. Through $(\nu a)$ creating fresh names at runtime and $\bar{a}\langle b \rangle$ sending them to other processes, the system can dynamically rewire its communication topology. Scope extrusion allows private channels to safely extend their scope along with message passing.

---

### Def-S-02-04. Binary Session Types

Honda's Session Types (1993) type-check session protocols, enabling compilers to verify that communicating endpoints "speak the same language" before runtime [^4][^5].

**Syntax**:

$$
\begin{aligned}
S, T ::= &\ !U.S \quad \text{(send value $U$, continue $S$)} \\
       |\ &\ ?U.S \quad \text{(receive value $U$, continue $S$)} \\
       |\ &\ \oplus\{l_1:S_1, \dots, l_n:S_n\} \quad \text{(internal choice)} \\
       |\ &\ \&\{l_1:S_1, \dots, l_n:S_n\} \quad \text{(external branch)} \\
       |\ &\ \mu t.S \quad \text{(recursive type)} \\
       |\ &\ t \quad \text{(type variable)} \\
       |\ &\ \text{end} \quad \text{(termination)}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Duality** $\overline{S}$: The dual of $!U.S$ is $?U.\overline{S}$; the dual of $\oplus$ is $\&$; $\overline{\text{end}} = \text{end}$.

**Intuition**: A binary session type is a "typed script" for bilateral communication, precisely prescribing who sends what, in which order, on which labels, and when to terminate. Duality is the mirror rule—if one endpoint says "I will send a string," the other must exactly say "I will receive a string."

## 2. Properties

### Lemma-S-02-01. Topological Invariance of Static Channel Models

For any process calculus without dynamic name creation and passing (e.g., CSP and CCS), the runtime communication topology is fully determined at the syntactic level and never changes during execution.

**Proof**: In CSP and CCS, the action set contains only predefined channel names and their complements. No $(\nu a)$ creation operator exists, and output values never include channel names. Therefore, the set of channels a process can use is invariantly determined by its initial free names. ∎

---

### Lemma-S-02-02. Turing Completeness of Dynamic Channel Calculi

Even monadic π-calculus with dynamic name creation $(\nu a)$ and name passing $\bar{a}\langle b \rangle$ is Turing-complete.

**Proof**: By the Church-Turing thesis, λ-calculus is Turing-complete. Milner (1992) proved that monadic π-calculus can encode λ-calculus: variable binding maps to name restriction $(\nu a)$; function application maps to request-response interaction over a freshly created channel; variable reference maps to "pointer" passing via channel names [^2]. ∎

---

### Prop-S-02-01. Duality Implies Communication Compatibility

If the session types at both ends of channel $c$ are $S$ and $\overline{S}$, then for every communication step, the sender's action and the receiver's action match perfectly in structure and value type.

**Proof**: By the duality function in Def-S-02-04, $!U.S$ dualizes to $?U.\overline{S}$, and $\oplus\{l_i:S_i\}$ dualizes to $\&\{l_i:\overline{S_i}\}$. Thus output always faces input, and label send always faces label receive. ∎

---

### Prop-S-02-02. Decidability of Finite-Control Static Calculi

For finite-control subsets of CSP and CCS (without replication $!$ or unbounded recursion), strong bisimulation (or failure equivalence) is decidable and PSPACE-complete.

**Proof**: When channels are static and syntax is finite, the reachable state space is finite. Christensen, Hüttel & Stirling (1995) proved PSPACE-completeness for finite CCS bisimulation [^8]. CSP's failures-divergences semantics is likewise decidable on finite-state subsets—this underpins industrial tools like FDR [^3]. ∎

## 3. Relations

### Relation 1: CSP $\perp$ CCS (Semantically Incomparable)

- **Semantic domain divergence**: CSP uses traces/failures/divergences; CCS uses bisimulation. Strong bisimulation strictly implies trace equivalence, but is incomparable with failure equivalence.
- **Termination observability**: CSP has successful termination SKIP ($\checkmark$); CCS does not. Any encoding from CSP to CCS must simulate $\checkmark$, but weak bisimulation conflates termination with deadlock.
- **Choice operators**: CSP distinguishes external $\square$ from internal $\sqcap$; CCS's $+$ cannot preserve this distinction under failure semantics.

**Conclusion**: CSP and CCS are semantically incomparable ($\perp$) in terms of equivalence semantics.

---

### Relation 2: CCS $\subset$ π-Calculus (Strict Inclusion)

- **Encoding existence**: CCS is a special case of π-calculus with mobility disabled ($\delta_{\text{mob}} = \text{static}$). All CCS SOS rules are restricted instances of π-calculus rules.
- **Separation result**: π-calculus supports dynamic channel creation and name passing. By Lemma-S-02-02, this makes it Turing-complete, while finite-control CCS is decidable. No faithful encoding from π to CCS exists [^6].

---

### Relation 3: CSP $\subset$ π-Calculus (Expressive Subsumption)

- **Encoding existence**: CSP's synchronous communication can be simulated by π-calculus request-reply patterns. Interleaving $|||$ maps to $\mid$; synchronized parallel $\parallel_A$ maps to shared-channel handshake; hiding maps to restriction.
- **Separation result**: CSP's communication topology is a syntactic invariant (Lemma-S-02-01), so runtime channel creation cannot be expressed.

---

### Relation 4: Session Types $\subset$ π-Calculus (Typed Subset)

- **Encoding existence**: Any binary session type can be encoded as a type-annotated π-calculus process. Session prefixes map directly to π input/output/label communication [^4].
- **Separation result**: π-calculus allows untyped or non-linear channel sharing, whereas session types enforce linear protocols. Processes like $(\nu c)(c\langle c \rangle.0 \mid c(x).x\langle v \rangle.0)$ violate linearity and cannot be described by any session type [^5].

## 4. Argumentation

### Argument 1: Why CSP and CCS Are Semantically Incomparable

CSP asks "how might this process behave in its environment?" (via refusal sets and divergence), while CCS asks "is this process indistinguishable from that one in any context?" (via bisimulation). These are answers to different questions.

Consider:
$$
P = a \to \text{STOP} \mathbin{\square} b \to \text{STOP}, \quad Q = a \to \text{STOP} \mathbin{\sqcap} b \to \text{STOP}
$$

In CSP failure semantics, $P \neq_F Q$ because $P$ can refuse $b$ when $a$ is chosen, whereas $Q$'s internal nondeterminism may lead to a branch that refuses $b$. In CCS, encoding both choices as $+$ makes them potentially equivalent under some bisimulation variants. This semantic misalignment prevents bidirectional faithful encoding.

---

### Argument 2: Why Dynamic Channels Strictly Enhance Expressiveness

Static channel calculi suffer from "topology freeze": all possible communication links are hardcoded in the program text. This is insufficient for microservices, P2P networks, or mobile agents, where connections must be established dynamically in response to external requests.

π-calculus breaks this limitation via name passing:
$$
(\nu a)(\bar{b}\langle a \rangle \mid a(x).P)
$$

A fresh channel $a$ is created and transmitted over existing channel $b$. The receiver, once holding $a$, can communicate with the sender over a previously non-existent private link. From a decidability perspective, this capability pushes the system from PSPACE-complete (L3) to Turing-complete (L4), making general deadlock detection and bisimulation undecidable.

---

### Argument 3: Session Types as a Disciplined Subset of π-Calculus

Session Types do not increase π-calculus expressiveness; instead, they **restrict** dangerous patterns (non-linear channel sharing, protocol reordering) through linear type constraints. This restriction buys two key guarantees:

1. **Communication Safety** (guaranteed by Prop-S-02-01): send and receive types/directions always match.
2. **Deadlock Freedom** (guaranteed by Cut elimination in linear logic): well-typed closed processes never deadlock [^4][^9].

Engineering-wise, Rust's ownership system is an effective implementation of these linear constraints.

## 5. Proofs

### Thm-S-02-01. Dynamic Channel Calculi Strictly Contain Static Channel Calculi

**Statement**: π-calculus is strictly more expressive than CSP and CCS. Faithful encodings exist from CSP → π and CCS → π, but not from π → CSP (or CCS).

**Proof**:

*Part 1: Encoding existence (CSP → π and CCS → π)*

CCS → π: Define $[\![ - ]\!]_{CCS}$ homomorphically, mapping $0$, $\alpha.P$, $P+Q$, $P \mid Q$, $P \setminus L$, and recursion directly to their π counterparts. All CCS SOS rules correspond to π rules under static mobility.

CSP → π: Encode STOP as $0$, SKIP as $0$ (with trace marker), $a \to P$ as input prefix, $\square$ as $+$, $\sqcap$ as $\tau.(\dots) + \tau.(\dots)$, $|||$ as $\mid$, and $\parallel_A$ as restriction over shared channels. Trace semantics are preserved.

*Part 2: Separation (π ↛ CSP/CCS)*

Assume a faithful encoding $[\![ - ]\!] : \pi \to \text{CSP}$. Consider:
$$
P_{\text{mob}} = (\nu a)(\bar{b}\langle a \rangle \mid a(x).\bar{c}\langle x \rangle)
$$

This process creates a fresh name $a$, sends it over $b$, and later uses $a$ to forward received values to $c$. CSP has no $(\nu a)$ operator to create new event names at runtime. Pre-allocating infinitely many names $\{a_1, a_2, \dots\}$ is non-compositional and yields infinite state spaces. Sangiorgi & Walker (2001) rigorously proved no congruence-preserving encoding from π to CCS exists [^6].

**Conclusion**: π-calculus $\supset$ CSP and π-calculus $\supset$ CCS. ∎

---

### Cor-S-02-01. Well-Typed Session Processes Are Deadlock-Free

**Statement**: Let $R = (\nu c_1:S_1)\dots(\nu c_n:S_n)(P_1 \mid \dots \mid P_m)$ be a closed, well-typed session process. Then $R$ either reduces to $0$ or there exists $R'$ such that $R \to R'$.

**Proof** (via Caires-Pfenning correspondence):

1. Session types correspond to linear logic propositions: $!U.S \leftrightarrow U \multimap S$, $?U.S \leftrightarrow U \otimes S$, $\oplus \leftrightarrow \oplus$, $\& \leftrightarrow \&$, end $\leftrightarrow \mathbf{1}$.
2. Process composition corresponds to proof composition (Cut rule).
3. Cut elimination guarantees any proof containing Cut can reduce; at the process level, communication in $(\nu c:S)(P \mid Q)$ can be progressively eliminated.
4. If $R$ were deadlocked, all subprocesses would be waiting with no possible action. In the linear logic correspondence, this would be an irreducible Cut, contradicting Cut elimination [^4][^9]. ∎

## 6. Examples

### Example 1: Dynamic Topology Change in π-Calculus

$$
P = (\nu a)(\bar{b}\langle a \rangle \mid a(x).\bar{c}\langle x \rangle \mid b(y).y\langle \text{msg} \rangle)
$$

1. Process $P$ creates fresh channel $a$ via $(\nu a)$.
2. Left branch $\bar{b}\langle a \rangle$ sends $a$ over public channel $b$.
3. Middle branch $a(x).\bar{c}\langle x \rangle$ waits on $a$, then forwards to $c$.
4. Right branch $b(y).y\langle \text{msg} \rangle$ receives $y$ (which is $a$), then sends msg over $y$.
5. Message $\text{msg}$ traverses the dynamically created channel $a$ and is forwarded to $c$.

This demonstrates runtime topology reconfiguration impossible in CSP/CCS.

---

### Example 2: CSP Synchronous Handshake

$$
P = a \to P', \quad Q = a \to Q', \quad R = P \parallel_{\{a\}} Q
$$

By [SYNC] rule, $P \parallel_{\{a\}} Q \xrightarrow{a} P' \parallel_{\{a\}} Q'$. Event $a$ is externally visible because it belongs to the synchronization set. With empty synchronization set $\emptyset$, $a$ actions of $P$ and $Q$ proceed independently.

---

### Example 3: Binary Session Type Protocol

Client-server protocol where client requests an integer and receives a boolean:

$$
S_{\text{client}} = !\text{Int}.?\text{Bool}.\text{end}
$$

Server dual:
$$
S_{\text{server}} = \overline{S_{\text{client}}} = ?\text{Int}.!\text{Bool}.\text{end}
$$

Process implementations:
$$
\begin{aligned}
P_{\text{client}} &= c!\langle 42 \rangle.c?(x).0 \\
P_{\text{server}} &= c?(y).c!\langle y > 0 \rangle.0
\end{aligned}
$$

The composition $(\nu c:S_{\text{client}})(P_{\text{client}} \mid P_{\text{server}})$ is well-typed and deadlock-free by Cor-S-02-01.

---

### Counter-Example 1: Session Type Violation Causes Deadlock

$$
P = c\langle v \rangle.c\langle w \rangle.0, \quad Q = c(y).0
$$

Composed as $(\nu c)(P \mid Q)$: $P$ sends $v$, $Q$ receives $v$ and terminates. $P$ then tries to send $w$, but no process is listening on $c$. Deadlock. In session types, $P$'s protocol is $!\text{T}.!\text{T}.\text{end}$ while $Q$'s is $?\text{T}.\text{end}$—the composition is rejected at compile time [^5].
